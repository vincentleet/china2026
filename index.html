🥣
Local canteen breakfast — jianbing & douzhir

Beijing's street breakfast culture is extraordinary and almost entirely missed by tourists. Jianbing (an egg and chilli crêpe cooked on a griddle in front of you) is the iconic one, but the real test for a foodie is douzhir — a fermented mung bean milk that Beijingers drink with fried dough rings. It's an acquired taste that divides opinion sharply, which is exactly why it's worth trying. Find it at a neighbourhood canteen before 9am. A genuinely authentic food experience that costs almost nothing.

One of China's finest museums and criminally undervisited compared to the Terracotta Warriors. Covers 1 million years of Shaanxi history — Zhou, Qin, Han, and Tang dynasties all had capitals here. Food — authentic & local
🍖
Yangrou paomo — local breakfast ritual

Xi'an's most iconic local breakfast — a rich mutton broth into which you tear apart and crumble a dense flatbread by hand (the tearing is done by you, not the kitchen, and the size of the pieces matters). The bowl then goes back to the kitchen to be finished with glass noodles and pickled garlic. Eaten at communal tables in no-frills canteens at 7am alongside locals who've been eating this way for generations. For a foodie this is the most authentic food experience in Xi'an — completely off most tourist itineraries and costs almost nothing.

Fresh biang biang noodles at a local canteen

For Vincent specifically, watching the making process is as interesting as the eating. Find it one street back from the main Muslim Quarter drag.

Sleep No More Shanghai — Punchdrunk immersive theatre

The Shanghai production of Punchdrunk's legendary immersive theatre show — running continuously since 2016 and considered by many fans to be the most fully realised version of the concept. Set in a 1930s Shanghai hotel across multiple floors, the audience wears masks and moves freely through the space while actors perform scenes simultaneously throughout. No dialogue — it's all movement, music, and atmosphere, so language is completely irrelevant. The noir 1930s Shanghai setting gains an extra layer of meaning when you're actually in Shanghai. Book well in advance — it sells out regularly. Critical tip: split up as a group at the start. The show is designed for solo exploration and following individual characters down corridors into private moments. Stay together and you'll miss most of what makes it extraordinary. If an actor takes you alone into a locked room for a one-on-one scene — go. It's the thing people are still talking about a year later.

Food — authentic & local
🦀
Hairy crab season — a once-a-year foodie event

Late October to early November is hairy crab season in Shanghai — one of the great seasonal eating events in Chinese food culture. Yangcheng Lake hairy crabs are steamed and eaten with ginger, vinegar, and Shaoxing rice wine in a ritual that takes 30–40 minutes per crab and involves a dedicated set of tiny tools. Locals book restaurants months in advance for this. For Vincent as a foodie, being in Shanghai at exactly the right time of year to eat the city's most celebrated seasonal dish is genuinely special. Ask your hotel to recommend a well-regarded local crab restaurant — not a tourist spot.

🥟
The xiaolongbao trail — comparing versions across the city

A proper foodie activity rather than a tourist tick — eating xiaolongbao at three or four different establishments across your Shanghai days and comparing the differences in skin thickness, broth intensity, and filling quality. Din Tai Fung is the famous benchmark but locals debate endlessly whether Jia Jia Tang Bao or Fu Chun does it better. Yang's Dumplings for the pan-fried version (sheng jian bao) is a completely different but equally essential comparison. Cheap enough to do multiple times across the trip without thinking about it.

🏘️
Longtang neighbourhood — authentic local Shanghai

The old lane house neighbourhoods (longtang) away from the French Concession tourist strip are where everyday Shanghai actually lives — elderly residents playing mahjong on the pavement, laundry strung between buildings, local wet market vendors, hole-in-the-wall noodle shops with three tables and one dish. The Jing'an and Putuo districts have well-preserved longtang that feel genuinely untouched. For Vincent's authentic experience interest this is the side of Shanghai that most visitors never find because it doesn't have a sign. Just walk in and wander.
